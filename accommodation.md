# Accommodation Options

> For two travelers (adult + 18-year-old), the sweet spot is usually a **private room at a guesthouse or business hotel** — you get privacy at hostel-adjacent prices when splitting the room.

## Hostels

**Price:** ¥2,000–5,000/bed in dorm (~$13–33); ¥6,000–12,000 for private rooms

**What to know:**
Japanese hostels are a different category from what you might expect elsewhere — clean, quiet, well-organized, often with excellent common areas and social spaces. Tokyo hostels in Asakusa, Shimokitazawa, and Shinjuku are particularly good.

**For two travelers:** Dorm beds mean no privacy and separate sleeping. A private room at a hostel brings the cost up to near business hotel level. Check if the private room rate is actually better than a budget business hotel before booking.

**Pros:**
- Cheapest option
- Great way to meet other travelers
- Common kitchens let you cook some meals

**Cons:**
- No privacy in dorms
- Shared bathrooms
- Can be noisy depending on location
- Private rooms are often small and similar in price to budget hotels

---

## Capsule Hotels

**Price:** ¥4,000–10,000/person (~$27–67)

**What to know:**
Modern capsule hotels (brands like 9 Hours, First Cabin, The Millennials) are a genuine Japan experience — not grimy closets but well-designed sleeping pods with personal lighting, small TV screens, decent mattresses, and noise-dampening curtains. Shared bathrooms are well-maintained. Coin lockers provided for luggage.

**Important:** Most capsule hotels have **gender-segregated floors** — men and women sleep on separate floors. If you and your cousin are different genders, you won't be on the same floor. You'll still share common areas (lobbies, restaurants, sometimes lounge floors).

**Some capsule hotels are male-only.** Check before booking.

**Recommended:**
- **9 Hours** (Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, Narita airport) — clean, modern, excellent design
- **First Cabin** — slightly larger "business class" capsules, feels more spacious
- **Dormy Inn** capsule-style floors — sometimes part of a larger business hotel property

**Pros:**
- Uniquely Japanese experience (do this at least once)
- Very affordable
- Usually in central locations

**Cons:**
- No storage space — use coin lockers at nearby train stations for large bags
- Some don't allow bags bigger than cabin luggage inside
- No real privacy
- Gender separation may matter for your travel pair

## Guesthouses / Minshuku

**Price:** ¥6,000–15,000/room for private room (~$40–100)

**What to know:**
Often family-run properties with a mix of private and dorm rooms. Common kitchen and lounge area. Some include a simple Japanese-style breakfast. Rooms may be traditional tatami style with futon on the floor, or Western-style beds — check when booking.

**Pros:**
- Best value for a private room
- Local atmosphere, usually owner on-site
- Often great for advice and recommendations
- Good way to experience everyday Japanese home hospitality

**Cons:**
- Less standardized — quality varies
- English communication may be limited
- Shared bathrooms are common even in private rooms
- Fewer amenities than hotels

## Business Hotels

**Price:** ¥10,000–20,000/room (~$67–133) — this is per **room**, not per person. Split between two = $33–67 each.

**What to know:**
Japan's business hotel chains are the backbone of comfortable budget-to-mid-range travel. Small rooms (Japanese hotel rooms are genuinely small by US standards) but impeccably clean, well-maintained, and consistently reliable. Most have coin laundry facilities. Many include simple breakfast options.

**Best chains:**
| Chain | What Makes It Good |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Dormy Inn** | Often has an onsen bath on premises. Excellent value. Ramen service at night (free or cheap). Strong recommendation. |
| **Toyoko Inn** | Includes simple breakfast. Locations near train stations everywhere. Very consistent. |
| **APA Hotel** | Extremely central locations. Smaller rooms but clean and functional. |
| **Super Hotel** | Good value, often organic breakfast included |
| **Richmond Hotel** | Slightly more space and comfort, mid-tier price |

**Pros:**
- Most reliable option — you know exactly what you're getting
- Good locations (near train stations)
- Full privacy
- Coin laundry on-site
- Dormy Inn locations often have onsen which is a significant bonus

**Cons:**
- Small rooms (twin rooms are tight for two adults)
- Not a uniquely Japanese experience
- Book early — central locations fill up fast, especially Golden Week

## Ryokan (Traditional Japanese Inn)

**Price:** ¥15,000–60,000+/person including dinner and breakfast

**The experience:**
This is the most culturally distinct accommodation option in Japan and worth doing for at least 1–2 nights. A ryokan is a traditional inn with tatami-mat rooms, futon bedding, yukata (cotton robe) provided and worn around the inn, and multi-course kaiseki meals served in your room or a dining hall. Most have communal onsen (hot spring baths) on premises.

**Etiquette:**
- Remove shoes at the genkan (entryway) and put on provided slippers
- No shoes on tatami — bare feet or socks only
- Yukata: left side over right (right-over-left is for funerals)
- Shower **before** entering the communal onsen bath — soap and shampoo stay in the shower area, not the onsen
- Onsen may be gender-segregated or have rotating times for private use
- Cash payment is standard at many traditional ryokan — bring yen
- Check-in is typically by 5–6 PM; dinner is scheduled, usually 6–7 PM

**Tattoos:** Some traditional ryokan (especially those with communal onsen) prohibit visible tattoos. If either traveler has tattoos, check the policy before booking or ask about private bath options.

**Best ryokan regions:**
- **Hakone** — Mt. Fuji views + onsen = classic Japan experience
- **Kinosaki Onsen** (near Kyoto) — entire town is a hot spring resort, walk between onsen in yukata
- **Nikko** — mountain forests, classic setting
- **Koyasan** — temple lodging variant (see below)
- **Kyoto** — many machiya (townhouse) style ryokan within the city

**Budget tip:** A budget ryokan without meals (¥8,000–15,000/person) still gives you the tatami room and onsen experience. Add dinner at a local restaurant. This is a good compromise if you want the experience without the full price.

**For your trip:** Budget at minimum one ryokan night. ¥20,000–40,000/person for a night with meals is the typical range for a solid experience. Two people = ¥40,000–80,000 for one night. It's expensive but it's the most memorable night of most Japan trips.

## Airbnb / Vacation Rentals

**Price:** Highly variable; ¥10,000–30,000/night for a whole apartment

**What to know:**
Airbnb is legal in Japan only if hosts are licensed under the Minpaku Law. Enforcement drove out a large percentage of listings in 2018 — supply is noticeably thinner than in other countries. Still available and can be excellent value for two people splitting an apartment with a kitchen.

**For Golden Week:** Prices surge significantly. Book very early if going this route.

**Pros:**
- Kitchen lets you cook and reduce food costs
- More space than a hotel room
- Can feel more like living in Japan than staying in a hotel

**Cons:**
- Less predictable than hotels
- Some areas restrict Airbnb (check listing details)
- Check-in logistics can be more complex

## Temple Lodging (Shukubo)

**Price:** ¥10,000–16,000/person including vegetarian meals (~$67–107)

**Best location: Koyasan (Mt. Koya)**

Stay overnight at a Buddhist temple complex. The experience includes:
- Simple tatami rooms
- Vegetarian shojin ryori (Buddhist cuisine) for dinner and breakfast
- Option to observe (not participate in) early morning monk prayers around 6 AM
- Wandering the sacred mountain at dawn when tour groups haven't arrived

Koyasan has about 50 temples that accept overnight guests. It's genuinely one of the most unique overnight experiences in Japan — quiet, atmospheric, completely different from the rest of the trip.

**How to get there:** Nankai Railway from Osaka (about 2 hours total including a cable car). Day-trippers flood in and leave by late afternoon — overnight guests have the place to themselves in the evening.

## Strategy for Your Trip

A suggested accommodation mix for a memorable trip at mid-range cost:

| Nights | City | Type | Why |
|--------|------|------|-----|
| 4–5 | Tokyo | Business hotel (Dormy Inn) | Central, reliable, often has onsen |
| 1 | Hakone | Ryokan with onsen | **The splurge night — do this** |
| 3–4 | Kyoto | Guesthouse or business hotel | Book early — Golden Week prices |
| 1–2 | Osaka | Business hotel | Cheaper than Kyoto, good base |
| 1 | Koyasan | Shukubo (temple lodging) | Unique experience worth adding |
| 1–2 | Hiroshima | Business hotel | Affordable, good locations |
