# Tips, Tricks & Practical Info

---

## The Suica Card — Most Important Thing to Get Right

A Suica is a rechargeable IC card that works for almost all transit and many purchases across Japan. Get one the moment you land.

**Where to get it:**
- JR ticket machines at Narita (NRT) or Haneda (HND) airports
- Requires ¥500 deposit (refunded when you return the card, minus ¥210 handling fee)
- Load ¥5,000 to start

**What it pays for:**
- Every subway, metro, bus, and train in Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, and most major cities
- Convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson)
- Vending machines
- Coin lockers at train stations
- Many taxis
- Some restaurants

**Top-up (charge):** At any convenience store or JR ticket machine. Takes 30 seconds.

**Apple Pay / Google Pay:** If your iPhone or Android supports it, add Suica to your wallet before you leave home. Top up with a credit card anywhere. This is the most frictionless option — no queuing at machines.

**At the end of your trip:** Use up most of the balance (it's easy to spend at convenience stores). Return at a JR office for the ¥500 deposit back minus ¥210 fee, or keep it for your next trip.

## Connectivity — eSIM vs. Pocket WiFi

**For two travelers in 2026: get two separate eSIMs.** It's cheaper and more convenient than sharing one pocket WiFi device.

**eSIM providers:**
| Provider | Plan | Cost |
|----------|------|------|
| Airalo | 20GB / 30 days | ~$18 |
| Saily | 15GB / 30 days | ~$15 |
| MobiMatter | 20GB / 30 days | ~$13 |
| T-Mobile | Included in some plans | Check your plan |

**Setup:** Buy and download before you leave. Activate when you land. Instant connectivity.

**Compatible phones:** Most phones from 2019 onward. US iPhones 14+ are eSIM-only — compatible. Check your phone's settings under Cellular > Add eSIM.

**Pocket WiFi** (from Ninja WiFi, Japan Wireless, etc.) makes more sense for 3+ travelers sharing. Starting at ~¥200–400/day. Adds a device to charge and carry.

**Use Google Maps offline:** Download Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka maps before you arrive. Google Maps is essential for Japan navigation — more accurate than Apple Maps for train routing.

## Cash — Still King in Japan

Japan is significantly more cash-dependent than the US. Budget to carry ¥5,000–15,000 in cash at all times.

**Cash-only situations you'll encounter:**
- Most traditional restaurants, ramen shops, izakayas
- Shrines and temples (entrance fees and vending)
- Traditional guesthouses and many ryokan
- Many taxis (though this is improving)
- Coin lockers, vending machines

**Best ATMs for foreign cards:**
- **7-Eleven ATMs** — Accept all major foreign cards, open 24/7, one inside every 7-Eleven convenience store (which are everywhere)
- **Japan Post ATMs** — Also reliable
- Most bank ATMs in Japan do NOT accept foreign cards

**Before you leave:** Notify your bank and credit card companies about international travel. Some banks charge foreign transaction fees (~3%) — consider getting a fee-free card (Charles Schwab debit, Chase Sapphire, Wise card).

**When paying cash:** Put the money in the small tray (torei) provided at the register. Don't hand cash directly to the cashier.

**Exchange tip:** You'll get good rates from a 7-Eleven ATM. Airport exchange counters are convenient but have slightly worse rates. Bring some USD just in case, but withdrawing yen on arrival is the simplest approach.

## Getting Around

### Within Cities — IC Card
Use your Suica for all subway, metro, and bus rides. Japan's city transit systems are extremely punctual and comprehensive. Tokyo's subway covers everywhere you'd want to go. Google Maps handles routing automatically.

### Between Cities — Shinkansen (Bullet Train)
The Shinkansen (高速鉄道) is fast, punctual to the minute, and comfortable. Tokyo to Kyoto takes 2 hours 15 minutes.

**Two types of seats:**
- **Reserved (shitei-seki):** Assigned seat, guaranteed. Book in advance, especially during Golden Week.
- **Unreserved (jiyū-seki):** Show up and sit in an unreserved car. Fine outside of peak travel times. During Golden Week, unreserved cars may be standing room only.

**Book via SmartEX app** (English available, slight discount vs. counter tickets, flexible changes). Or at any JR ticket counter.

**Luggage on Shinkansen:** Large bags (bigger than 160cm total dimensions) now require a reservation to place in the dedicated luggage space at the rear of each car. Book this when reserving your ticket. No extra cost but required.

### Luggage Forwarding (Takkyubin)
One of Japan's most useful traveler services: ship your luggage ahead to your next hotel for ¥1,500–2,000 per bag. Drop off at your current hotel desk or any convenience store the morning before you travel. It arrives at your next hotel by the following day (sometimes same day).

**Why this is great:** Travel day with just a day bag instead of rolling heavy luggage through crowded Shinkansen stations. Essential if visiting Kyoto and Nara on the same day.

**Service providers:** Yamato Transport (Kuroneko) and Sagawa are most common. Hotels can arrange this — just ask at the front desk the night before.

### Coin Lockers
At every major train station, coin lockers hold your bag for ¥300–700/day depending on size. Small lockers accept IC card payment. Great for stashing your bag on days you're changing hotels or doing a day trip.

## Food

### Convenience Stores Are Not a Compromise
Japanese convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) are a cultural institution. The food is genuinely good:
- Onigiri (rice balls) — ¥120–180, dozens of fillings
- Hot foods: steamed buns, fried chicken, corn dogs
- Sandwiches, fresh sushi sets, hot noodles
- Pastries and desserts that are better than most US bakeries
- Hot coffee from the machine at the register (¥100–200)

A full convenience store meal costs ¥400–700. Do this regularly — it's not "settling," it's the experience.

### Set Lunches (Teishoku)
Many sit-down restaurants offer lunch sets where you get the same food as dinner for 30–50% less. A ¥2,500 dinner becomes a ¥1,200 lunch set. Eat your big meal at lunch.

### Standing Restaurants
Ramen shops, soba/udon counters, and gyudon (beef bowl) chains with counter seating are fast, cheap (¥500–900), and often excellent. You'll see them at every train station.

### Gyudon Chains (Yoshinoya, Sukiya, Matsuya)
Beef bowl over rice for ¥500–700. Open 24 hours at many locations. Good emergency food when everything else is closed or too crowded.

### Supermarket Bento Discount
Supermarkets (look for LIFE, Aeon, Daiei, local chains) mark down prepared bento boxes and deli items 20–50% after 7–8 PM. Yellow discount stickers. Stack them up.

### What to Eat and Where

| Food | Where to Get It | Cost |
|------|----------------|------|
| Ramen | Dedicated ramen shops | ¥900–1,500 |
| Sushi | Conveyor belt (kaiten-zushi) | ¥110–330/plate |
| Tempura | Lunch set at tempura restaurants | ¥1,000–2,000 |
| Tonkatsu (breaded pork cutlet) | Tonkatsu restaurants | ¥1,000–2,000 |
| Yakiniku (grilled meat) | Yakiniku restaurants | ¥2,000–5,000 |
| Yakitori (grilled skewers) | Yakitori stalls, izakayas | ¥150–400/skewer |
| Takoyaki | Dotonbori (Osaka), street stalls | ¥500–800 for 8 balls |
| Okonomiyaki | Osaka or Hiroshima restaurants | ¥800–1,500 |
| Matcha everything | Kyoto, especially Uji | ¥300–800 |
| Onsen egg | Hakone (cooked in volcanic hot springs) | ¥250–500 |

### Dietary Restrictions
Japan is difficult for strict vegetarians and vegans. Dashi (fish stock) is in almost everything including seemingly vegetarian soups and sauces. Tell restaurants you need shojin ryori (Buddhist vegetarian) or use a translation card explaining your restrictions. Convenience stores label vegetarian items. Famously, temple lodging (shukubo) at Koyasan serves excellent fully vegetarian meals.

## Language

**You don't need to speak Japanese to have a great trip — but a few things help:**

**Essential phrases:**
| Phrase | Pronunciation | Use |
|--------|--------------|-----|
| Sumimasen | soo-mi-mah-sen | Excuse me / Sorry (use constantly) |
| Arigatou gozaimasu | ah-ree-gah-toh go-zai-mas | Thank you (formal) |
| Kore wo kudasai | ko-reh wo koo-dah-sai | This one please (while pointing) |
| Ikura desu ka? | ee-koo-rah des-kah | How much is it? |
| Eigo ga wakarimasu ka? | ay-go gah wah-kah-ree-mas kah | Do you understand English? |
| Toire wa doko desu ka? | toy-reh wah doh-koh des kah | Where is the bathroom? |

**Apps:**
- **Google Translate** — Download Japanese for offline use. Camera translation (point at menus/signs) is excellent and works offline with the downloaded pack.
- **DeepL** — Better for nuanced translation of longer text
- **Google Maps** — Download offline maps for Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka before you arrive. Transit routing is very accurate.

## Etiquette Basics

**Shoes:**
- Remove at the entrance (genkan) of ryokan, traditional guesthouses, some traditional restaurants, and any home
- Look for a step up in the floor and a cubby for shoes — that's your cue
- Slippers will usually be provided; use them in the hallways
- Remove slippers before stepping onto tatami mat — bare feet or socks on tatami only
- There will be separate bathroom slippers — only wear them in the bathroom

**On trains and subways:**
- No phone calls — text and message are fine
- Keep your voice low
- If you eat (you generally shouldn't), be discreet
- Priority seats (usually a different color) near doors are for elderly, pregnant, and disabled — give them up

**Queuing:**
- Japan has exceptional queuing culture — lines form for trains, attractions, and escalators (stand on left, walk on right in Tokyo; opposite in Osaka)
- Do not cut in line

**Chopstick etiquette:**
- Don't stick chopsticks upright into rice — this is only done at funerals
- Don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick — also a funeral custom
- It's fine to eat with a spoon; ask by miming

**Temples and shrines:**
- Speak quietly
- Don't climb on or touch statues
- Wash hands at the purification fountain (temizuya) before entering a shrine — right hand first, then left, then rinse mouth (optional)
- Photography is usually fine outdoors; interiors often have no-photo signs

**Tipping:**
- **Do not tip.** Not at restaurants, not at hotels, not in taxis. Japan has a no-tipping culture — it can be awkward or mildly insulting. The price is the price.
- Exception: private tour guides for multi-day personal tours sometimes accept tips, placed in a small envelope and given with two hands.

**Tattoos:**
- Traditional onsen and some ryokan prohibit visible tattoos. If either traveler has tattoos, check policies before booking, or ask about private bath options (many ryokan have private baths available for an extra fee).

**Trash:**
- Public trash cans are extremely rare in Japan. You will be carrying your convenience store wrappers for a while. Some 7-Elevens have bins near the entrance for their own packaging.
- Don't leave trash on the street — carry a small bag.

## Seasonal Highlights — Late April / Early May

### Cherry Blossoms (Honest Assessment)
Peak cherry blossom in Tokyo and Kyoto is late March to early April — you'll likely miss full peak. However:
- **Late-blooming varieties** (yaezakura, double-petal cherries) are still blooming in Tokyo through late April in parks like Shinjuku Gyoen
- **Mountain areas** (Hakone, Takayama, Kanazawa) are at or near peak late April — timing aligns with your trip
- **Northern Japan** (Sendai, Aomori, Hokkaido) sees peak bloom late April through early May

### What IS Blooming Late April – Early May

| Flower | Where | When |
|--------|-------|------|
| **Wisteria (fuji)** | Ashikaga Flower Park, Kawachi Wisteria Garden | Late April–mid May |
| **Azaleas (tsutsuji)** | Nezu Shrine (Tokyo), many temple gardens | Late April–May |
| **Nemophila (baby blue eyes)** | Hitachi Seaside Park | Late April–early May |
| **Fresh green (shinryoku)** | All forests and temple gardens | Early May onward |

**Wisteria at Ashikaga Flower Park** is the standout seasonal experience for your exact dates — enormous pale purple wisteria tunnels that look like something from a fantasy film. Timed entry tickets required; buy in advance.

### Weather
| Region | Temperature | Conditions |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Tokyo | 16–22°C (61–72°F) day / 11–15°C nights | Mostly sunny, occasional spring rain |
| Kyoto | 18–24°C (64–75°F) | Very pleasant; can be warm midday |
| Osaka | Similar to Kyoto, slightly warmer | |
| Hakone | 12–18°C (54–64°F) | Can be cool and misty |
| Hiroshima | 19–23°C (66–73°F) | Lovely |

Rainy season (tsuyu) starts mid-to-late June — you're safely ahead of it.

### What to Pack
- Light layers — mornings and evenings cool; midday can be warm
- Compact umbrella — spring showers happen
- **Comfortable walking shoes** — you will walk 15,000–25,000 steps/day without exception
- **Slip-on shoes** — easy to remove for ryokan and traditional restaurants
- Small day backpack — for daily essentials; leave main luggage at hotel or use takkyubin
- Portable battery pack — Google Maps + translation drain battery fast
- Cash pouch or money belt — for carrying yen securely
- Light rain jacket — doubles as extra layer in mountain areas
- Sunscreen — full days outdoors in spring sun
